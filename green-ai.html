Step 05 // A better path

What Green AI
Looks Like

Fighting harmful projects is half the work. The other half is knowing what to demand instead. Genuinely sustainable infrastructure is possible; here’s what it requires.

A quick, honest caution first: companies love to announce sustainability. The lesson from this whole project is to never trust a goal until you see the physical action behind it. So treat each item below as something to verify and demand, not something to assume a company is already doing.

The building blocks of green AI

01

Closed-loop & water-free cooling

Instead of evaporating fresh drinking water, closed-loop systems recirculate the same coolant, and emerging water-free designs cut potable-water draw dramatically. This directly attacks the water-stress harm from Step 03.

02

Waste-heat recovery

The enormous heat these facilities produce doesn’t have to be wasted. Captured and piped into municipal district-heating networks, it can warm nearby homes and buildings, turning a pollutant into a public benefit.

03

Circular hardware (the 4 Rs)

Rather than shredding servers after a few years, a circular model (maintain, refurbish, reuse, recycle) extends hardware life and reclaims components, cutting both e-waste and the demand for newly mined critical minerals.

04

Real transparency (the Digital Product Passport)

Emerging EU rules like the Digital Product Passport would require hardware to carry a verifiable record of its material origins, repairability, and disposal, making greenwashing far harder and giving communities real data to hold companies to.

The bottom line

Green AI isn’t about waiting for corporations to do the right thing. The Jevons Paradox shows efficiency alone won’t save us. It’s about communities knowing exactly what good infrastructure looks like, then using the tools in Step 04 to demand it as a condition of building at all.

That’s the guide.
