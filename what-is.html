Step 01 // Understand

What Is an AI
Data Center?

Before you can fight what’s being built next to your town, you have to know what it actually is. Start here - no technical background needed.

Definition

An AI data center is a specialized warehouse-scale facility built to run artificial intelligence. Unlike a traditional data center - which uses standard processors (CPUs) for everyday computing - an AI data center is custom-built for high-performance computing: training and running huge models that constantly ingest and process enormous volumes of data.

Why it’s different from a “normal” data center

Standard infrastructure would be overwhelmed by AI workloads almost instantly. To keep up, AI facilities are packed with specialized, power-hungry hardware and run at an intensity ordinary computing never reaches. That intensity is the root of every downstream problem in this guide - the water, the grid strain, the land use. The hardware demands explain the harm.

The building blocks

01

GPUs & TPUs - the engines

Graphics Processing Units (GPUs) and Tensor Processing Units (TPUs) are specialized AI accelerators. They use parallel processing - breaking huge problems into many small pieces solved at once - which is what makes deep learning possible. The catch: they run extremely hot and consume vastly more electricity per cycle than the CPUs in a normal computer.

02

High-speed storage & memory

AI models need data delivered fast enough to keep those GPUs from sitting idle. That means advanced storage like NVMe solid-state drives and High-Bandwidth Memory (HBM) - semiconductor hardware with no moving parts, built for speed and volume.

03

Cooling systems

All that hardware generates enormous heat. Ordinary air conditioning often isn’t enough, so facilities turn to liquid cooling - circulating water or coolant directly past the hardware. This is where the water consumption you’ll see in the next section comes from.

04

Scale: “hyperscale”

The industry runs on hyperscale facilities - campuses housing thousands of servers across hundreds of thousands of square feet. This is the physical footprint that lands on real towns, farmland, and watersheds.

Now you know what it is.
