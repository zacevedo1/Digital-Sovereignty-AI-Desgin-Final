Yes - a student made this, and used AI to build it. This page shows the research, the process, and an honest accounting of how AI shaped the outcome. Made by Zahara Acevedo for AI + Design Studio.

01

THE INQUIRY

Where the question started - and where it ended up.

Core research question

How can we translate the physical hazards of AI infrastructure (grid strain, water depletion, noise pollution) and local legal choke points (zoning moratoriums, FOIA, PUC interventions) into an accessible, interactive toolkit that equips citizens to proactively defend their land against Big Tech?

My starting point was much broader and more passive. I began by investigating “Green AI Data Centers” - how tech corporations could shift to sustainable energy - essentially waiting for the industry to fix itself.

The pivot - the Jevons Paradox

Discovering the Jevons Paradox changed everything. As infrastructure becomes more efficient, corporations scale operations fast enough to erase the savings. That’s why the sector hasn’t truly gone green - and why I pivoted from waiting on corporate sustainability to building local legal-defense tools. The whole site reflects that shift: it doesn’t ask companies to be better, it equips communities to demand it.

02

RESEARCH &
PROCESS

How the research was done, and how the AI was steered.

Workflow timeline

  1. WEEK 6

    Establishing the “why”

    Built a foundational understanding of how AI data centers affect environment and economy. Logged 26 sources via NotebookLM deep research (~3 hours). Decided early to slightly narrow what the project was trying to solve.

  2. WEEK 7

    Sustainable vs. “green” AI

    Recognized the project was more about learning than building. Noticed prompts hallucinated when under-specified - answers usually arrived 2–3 chats later once the request was precise.

  3. WEEK 8

    Closed-loop cooling & embodied carbon

    Studied closed-loop design and embodied carbon. Microsoft’s sustainability reports clarified what counts as good transparency - and how to double-check it.

  4. WEEK 9 - THE PIVOT

    From the outside in, to the root

    Realized that if the root process of digital economic growth doesn’t change, nothing changes. Pivoted to advocacy: what communities can do to halt corporate and local-government decisions. Published the GitHub command center.

How I verified sources

I treated corporate data not as fact but as a structure to reverse-engineer. The Microsoft Sustainability Report was a key baseline - even knowing corporate numbers can be spun, analyzing polished reports taught me what to look for when spotting false figures and greenwashing. The throughline: never trust a sustainability goal until you see the physical action behind it.

A note on numbers

Many viral AI-footprint stats - “X bottles of water per query” - trace back to estimates repeated far beyond what their sources actually claim. Applying this project’s own standard, I chose not to publish precise figures I couldn’t trace to a primary source. The learning pages describe the threat without leaning on numbers I can’t stand behind.

Prompt experiment (Gemini Pro)

01 - “The Cost of a Prompt” Calculator

Asked the model to build an interactive calculator with a slider (1–100 prompts/day) updating water, rare-earth, and embodied-carbon metrics in real time.

03

AUTHORSHIP
& AI

An honest account of what I made and what the AI did.

How I kept authorship

I strictly managed the AI coding agent’s focus. When it tried to fold curiosity-driven tangents into the actual project, I told it “no.” The AI was a coding assistant, not an art director. The structure, content hierarchy, and boundaries were mine.

What the AI shaped

I fed it many visual references; it suggested the term “Tactical Terminal,” which captured the design intent I already had but lacked words for. I also deliberately refused AI-generated images - this site uses only real, verified photography, because the physical reality is the whole point.

The honest contradiction

This site’s structure was built with AI - a demonstration of how powerful these tools are. But that doesn’t erase the environmental cost the site documents. Both things are true at once, and naming that tension is exactly why the fight for transparency and community rights matters.

04

REFLECTION

What changed in my thinking - and what’s next.

My biggest shift was learning to pick at an issue from its core. Solving from the outside - appealing to corporate goodwill - changes nothing if the root process of digital economic growth stays the same. That moved the project from passive observation to active defense: equipping people with the legal and visual tools to demand transparency and protect their own resources.

I also learned to steer AI intentionally, setting firm boundaries so the project stayed concentrated instead of drifting toward whatever tangent the model found interesting. And I learned to distrust a clean number until I can trace it - a habit this project forced on me and that I built into the site itself.

If I had another ten weeks, I’d expand this into a live, localized intelligence system: an early-warning feed for data-center scouting, an interactive map for residents to report real-time impacts, and a directory connecting users directly to active grassroots advocacy groups - moving the toolkit from education to ongoing, real-time community defense.
