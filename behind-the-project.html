Behind the project

The Work
Behind It

Yes, a student made this, and used AI to build it. This page shows the research, the process, and an honest accounting of how AI shaped the outcome. Made by Zahara Acevedo for AI + Design.

01

THE INQUIRY

Where the question started and where it ended up.

Core research question

How can we translate the physical hazards of AI infrastructure (grid strain, water depletion, noise pollution) and local legal choke points (zoning moratoriums, FOIA, PUC interventions) into an accessible, interactive toolkit that equips citizens to proactively defend their land against Big Tech?

My starting point was much broader and more passive. I began by investigating “Green AI Data Centers,” how tech corporations could shift to sustainable energy, essentially waiting for the industry to fix itself.

The pivot: the Jevons Paradox

Discovering the Jevons Paradox changed everything. As infrastructure becomes more efficient, corporations scale operations fast enough to erase the savings. That’s why the sector hasn’t truly gone green, and why I pivoted from waiting on corporate sustainability to building local legal-defense tools. The whole site reflects that shift: it doesn’t ask companies to be better, it equips communities to demand it.

02

RESEARCH &
PROCESS

How the research was done, and how the AI was steered.

Workflow timeline

  1. WEEK 6

    Establishing the “why”

    Built a foundational understanding of how AI data centers affect environment and economy. Logged 26 sources via NotebookLM deep research (~3 hours). Decided early to slightly narrow what the project was trying to solve.

    This part of the project helped me understand the broader context and scope of the issue, which led to the building of "what it consumes" in terms of water, energy, grid, and the overall impact on communities.

  2. WEEK 7

    Sustainable vs. “green” AI

    Recognized the project was more about learning than building. Noticed prompts hallucinated when under-specified; answers usually arrived 2–3 chats later once the request was precise.

    While I did originally plan to build a comprehensive tool, the iterative process revealed the need for a more focused approach, and so I shifted my focus to creating an educational resource that would empower communities to demand change.

  3. WEEK 8

    Closed-loop cooling & embodied carbon

    Studied closed-loop design and embodied carbon. Microsoft’s sustainability reports clarified what counts as good transparency, and how to double-check it.

    This research was the foundation for the "Green AI" section, which outlines the building blocks of sustainable AI infrastructure, and why sustainable practices need to be verified, not just promised.

  4. WEEK 9: THE PIVOT

    From the outside in, to the root

    Realized that if the root process of digital economic growth doesn’t change, nothing changes. Pivoted to advocacy: what communities can do to halt corporate and local-government decisions. Published the GitHub command center.

    This was the pivotal moment that shifted the project's focus to building a toolkit for empowering communities with the knowledge to demand change and take action.

How I verified sources

I treated corporate data not as fact but as a structure to reverse-engineer. The Microsoft Sustainability Report was a key baseline, even knowing corporate numbers can be spun, analyzing polished reports taught me what to look for when spotting false figures and greenwashing. The throughline: never trust a sustainability goal until you see the physical action behind it.

A note on numbers

Many viral AI-footprint stats, “X bottles of water per query,” trace back to estimates repeated far beyond what their sources actually claim. Applying this project’s own standard, I chose not to publish precise figures I couldn’t trace to a primary source. The learning pages describe the threat without leaning on numbers I can’t stand behind.

Prompt experiment (Gemini Pro)

01: “The Cost of a Prompt” Calculator

Asked the model to build an interactive calculator with a slider (1–100 prompts/day) updating water, rare-earth, and embodied-carbon metrics in real time. The outcome became the interactive scale visualizer on the home page, adapted to show relative levels rather than precise figures in keeping with the project's stance on unverifiable numbers.

03

AUTHORSHIP
& AI

Five moments where I stopped asking the AI for help and started directing it.

Throughout this project, my trajectory shifted from passive recipient of AI-generated content to active architect and strategist of my work. I stopped letting the AI define the boundaries and started defining them myself. Here are the specific moments where that happened.

01

The “click moment” of critical skepticism

I didn’t just accept the AI’s early summaries as truth. When the information felt like “too good to be true,” specifically around corporate transparency, I forced a fact-check. I demanded evidence and explicitly rejected the “happy path” the AI was feeding me. This was the moment I stopped treating the AI as an authority and started treating it as a tool to be interrogated.

02

The strategic pivot to physicality

I fundamentally changed the project’s direction by rejecting the abstract notion of the “Cloud.” I pushed the conversation away from software and toward the brutal physical reality of resource extraction: water, energy, and hardware waste. By forcing the AI to engage with the Jevons Paradox and corporate secrecy, I moved the project from a generic report to a hard-hitting investigation of systemic issues.

03

Owning the “how” and “why”: legal sovereignty

I identified the gap between understanding a problem and solving it. I insisted I wasn’t just here to document harm, but to arm communities with a legal arsenal. By bringing the 10th Amendment and community legal powers (CBAs, FOIA, zoning moratoriums) into the conversation, I steered the project toward a concrete, actionable toolkit rather than a passive educational site.

04

Taking control of the build

I stopped the research drift by forcing the AI to align with my submission requirements. I insisted it act as a collaborator to help me write in my own voice rather than generate generic copy. I rejected the “chaotic hacker” visual direction and demanded a serious, trustworthy civic-defender aesthetic that respects the audience’s intelligence. The one thing I took directly from the AI was the name: when I described the visual direction I was going for, it offered “Tactical Terminal” as a label, which perfectly captured the design intent I already had but lacked the exact words for. That name became the anchor for every visual decision on the site. When I recognized I was behind on the actual building, I took control by asking for modular prompts and a weekly roadmap that reflected my actual progress.

05

Setting an editorial line the AI kept trying to cross

The AI kept offering precise per-prompt figures: “X bottles of water per query,” “Y watt-hours per prompt.” They sounded convincing, but when I traced them back the sources didn’t actually support the numbers. So I stopped using them. It wasn’t a grand decision, just the realization that repeating a statistic I couldn’t verify would undercut everything else on the site. The harder part was that those numbers would have made the argument easier to land. I left them out anyway.

In short

I moved from a student asking an AI for help to a researcher directing an AI to build a specific, high-stakes advocacy product. I chose the harder path, focusing on physical and legal reality, and that is exactly what gave this project its real teeth.

The original scope included an interactive FOIA generator, a Corporate Transparency Scorecard, and a physical threat index. Those ambitions shaped the final build: the legal toolkit accordions are the generator, the sourced proof cards are the scorecard, and the scale visualizer on the home page is the threat index, each adapted to the project's standard of not publishing figures I couldn't verify.

The honest contradiction

This site’s structure was built with AI, a demonstration of how powerful these tools are. But that doesn’t erase the environmental cost the site documents. Both things are true at once, and naming that tension is exactly why the fight for transparency and community rights matters.

04

REFLECTION

What changed in my thinking and what’s next.

My biggest shift was learning to pick at an issue from its core. Solving from the outside, appealing to corporate goodwill, changes nothing if the root process of digital economic growth stays the same. That moved the project from passive observation to active defense: equipping people with the legal and visual tools to demand transparency and protect their own resources.

I also learned to steer AI intentionally, setting firm boundaries so the project stayed concentrated instead of drifting toward whatever tangent the model found interesting. And I learned to distrust a clean number until I can trace it, a habit this project forced on me and that I built into the site itself.

If I had another ten weeks, I’d expand this into a live, localized intelligence system: an early-warning feed for data-center scouting, an interactive map for residents to report real-time impacts, and a directory connecting users directly to active grassroots advocacy groups, moving the toolkit from education to ongoing, real-time community defense.

This project was a choice I made the moment I joined my AI+ Design class, and through the 4-5 weeks I spent working on it, my overall goal remained the same, to make a site to educate those who want to learn and put in the time to learn how to fight against corporate giants. However at the end of the day, I'm only a design student who cares deeply about the impact AI has on the community and environment, and all sources I used are linked in the sources section below, and while I do provide many examples with credible sources, please always make sure to also do your own research.
